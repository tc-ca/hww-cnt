Running the play

A critical path is a document similar in use to a timeline; however, it is much more in-depth. These documents can be visualized as charts or maps, but the key concept is to show the evolution of a project through creation / development, approval, launch, and other phases. A critical path shows a clear decision on who is responsible for a portion of a project, how long it will take, when it needs to be done by, and other important information. Critical paths are also highly useful for recurring tasks, such as monthly reports.

The process to create a critical path involves planning stages – identifying important dates, key players, and what we call “slow-downs” – and ends with putting it all together in a map or chart; whichever method works best for your team. A critical path can help your team adjust to new deadlines and prioritize important tasks or projects. Closely tied to the Conduct a dependency mapping session play, it can help your team to notice the bigger picture of a project, and it could help identify new areas where your team can slim down a process and make future projects run much more smoothly.

Identify important dates

The first step in creating a critical path is to identify the most important dates.

It is wise to segment your project into workable chunks. You can do this by breaking it down into approval stages, drafting stages, stages where the project may change hands, or any other method that is appropriate for your project (or a combination of these methods). The questions listed below will help you determine any due dates you should consider in your critical path:

Important Dates:

    li>When should the project be started?
  • When is the project due?
  • Does this project require approval(s)?
  • Will the project be switching leads or analysts?
  • Is the project naturally separated into sections, stages, or segments?
  • What happens to the project once it is done?

Tip

If you’re having trouble narrowing down aspects of your project, try any of these plays to help bring it into a manageable scope or something that is easy to break down into stages.

  • Conduct a project kick-off session
  • Clarify objectives and key results (OKR)
  • Define success with a project poster

Key players

The next step in a critical path is to identify all key players in the project’s timeline. Look at the questions below, and log your answers on a piece of paper or separate document:

  • Who will be the primary project holder?
  • Who will assist the project holder?
  • Who needs to approve the project?
  • Are there any external stakeholders involved? If so, who?
  • Who will be responsible for the project once it is over?

“Slow-downs”

There are several other aspects to consider in creating a critical path, which we label as “slow-downs”. These are things that unfortunately must add time to your project, such as:

  • The ADM approving the project proposal requires three days to approve, and wishes to see any changes made before proceeding;
  • The analyst responsible for drafting the project costing analysis is inundated with other tasks;
  • The project lead is on vacation for a week during the project’s launch phase.

It is therefore very important that these slow-downs are noted and incorporated into the critical path before beginning your project. Some slow-downs can be mitigated by moving duties to another person, such as if an analyst is inundated with other tasks or if the project lead is on vacation. However, in situations where tasks cannot be handed to someone else, the best option is to incorporate plenty of time into the critical path. For example, if the ADM requires three days to approve and wishes to see any changes, you could schedule one week or more for that task.

Be sure to identify any slow-downs and make note of how long they may take.

Tip

Are you noticing a lot of slow-downs on your critical path? Use plays such as the Conduct a dependency mapping session to find methods to get rid of them.

Putting it all together

So you have all this information about due dates, approval times, everyone who is working on the project, and you have identified any issues that may slow progress. Now what? It’s time to put together your critical path. There are several ways to do this via charts or maps. It is also easy to customize your critical path to make it as simple or detail-oriented as you wish.
